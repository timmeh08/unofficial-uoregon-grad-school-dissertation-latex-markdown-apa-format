This thesis describes two searches for new physics with the ATLAS detector at the LHC, with the focus of discovering or excluding dark matter signals.
The search for dark matter and BSM physics will continue to be a major focus of the ATLAS physics program in the coming years. 
By setting new and competitive limits on the production of dark mesons and dark photons, the dark sector phase space is further constrained, and the results of these searches will be used to guide future searches for dark sectors at the LHC and beyond.\par
% summarize dark meson findings here in new parapgraph
The dark meson search utilized the full Run 2 dataset of $139~\ifb$ of $pp$ collisions at $\sqrt{s}=13$ TeV, collected by the ATLAS experiment, and focused on the all-hadronic and one-lepton final states.
Though no excess above the SM was observed in the Run 2 dark meson search, the strongest limits to date are obtained from the one-lepton channel.
For SU(2)$_L$ signals with $m_{\pi_D}/m_{\rho_D} = .45$, dark pions with masses below 940 GeV are excluded at 95\% CL, while for $m_{\pi_D}/m_{\rho_D} = .25$, the exclusion extends up to 740 GeV.
The dark meson results remain the first and most stringent limit on this type of model, extending the exclusion reach significantly compared to previous re-interpretations of LHC searches for similar signatures.
A Run 3 analysis has the potential to extend the exclusion reach with a dataset that is over twice as large as the Run~2 dataset.
With the large-R jet identification in the gFEX, the all-hadronic channel might be able to use direct triggering on boosted topologies, and lower the large $H_T$ requirement that cut out a siginificant fraction of the signal in the Run 2 analysis.
A lower $H-T$ requirement has the potential to increase signal acceptance by up to 30\% for lower $m_{\pi_D}/m_{\rho_D}$ ratios and lower dark pion masses.
With generic multi-jet events being filtered more efficiently, combined with a larger dataset, a revised all-hadronic channel may be able to reach sensitivities comparable to the one-lepton channel.

The dark photon search was performed using Run-3 $pp$ collision data at $\sqrt{s}=13.6$ TeV, collected by the ATLAS experiment in 2023 and 2024, corresponding to an integrated luminosity of $134.6~\ifb$. 
For the dark photon search, the results of the Run 3 analysis represent the most stringent limits on the branching ratio of Higgs to a photon and dark photon to date, with an observed (expected) upper limit of 1.10\% (0.94\%) at 95\% CL, superseding the current best limits of 1.3\% (1.5\%) from the ATLAS Run-2 combination~\cite{ATLAS:2024cju}. Combining this analysis with the Run-2 results, an observed (expected) upper limit of 0.82\% (0.77\%) at 95\% CL is achieved.
The inclusion of the ggF channel, which is an order of magnitude larger production cross section than the VBF and ZH channels, was made possible by the implementation of a new composite trigger that allows lower thresholds on the photon transverse momentum and \ETmiss, greatly increasing the acceptance of ggF events.
This allowed the search to reach a previously unexplored phase space down to $\ETmiss > ~100$ GeV and $\pTy > 50$ GeV. 
A natural next step for this search is to include the full Run 3 dataset, which is expected to reach an integrated luminosity of around $300~\ifb$; more than double the dataset used in this search. 
While the trained vertex BDT was effective at suppressing events with misidentified primary vertices, the analysis would greatly benefit from improvements in the vertex reconstruction and identification algorithms, which would further reduce the background and increase the sensitivity of the search as discussed in Section \ref{sec:vertex-tagging}.
In addition, a newly optimized signal region combining machine learning techniques with multi-dimensional phase space could further improve the sensitivity of the search, especially in the ggF channel where the kinematics are more challenging.\par %forgot to add the LLP version
% summarize upgrade work + HL-LHC and future prospects here
The HL-LHC will provide an unprecedented amount of data, with an expected integrated luminosity above $3000~\ifb$.
This will allow for significant improvements in the sensitivity of dark sector searches.
In addition, the ATLAS detector will undergo significant upgrades to cope with the increased luminosity and pile-up conditions.
Among the upgrades, the new Global Trigger will allow for offline-like algorithms to be implemented at the L0 Trigger level.
The work discussed in Chapter \ref{ch:hl-lhc-atlas-upgrades} on the development of the USCA topological clustering algorithm will allow for significant improvements in object reconstruction, including for jets and taus, at the L0 Trigger level.
With efficient and dynamic firmware algorithms within the Global Trigger, as well as the multiple improvements in the detector subsystems, ATLAS will be able to increase the sensitivity of dark sector searches and explore new areas of BSM phase space in the years to come.
