
The trigger used in the analysis is a combination of requirements on trigger-level cuts on $p_T^\gamma,~E_T^{miss},~m_T$, that correspond to analogous cuts on the offline variables, according to Table~\ref{tab:trig_offline_cuts}.

In addition to the selection reported in table \ref{tab:trig_offline_cuts}, all preselections listed in section \ref{sec:presel} are applied in the following studies.

The efficiency of the trigger selection with respect to the offline selection, defined as
\begin{equation}
  \epsilon(\textrm{trigger}|\textrm{offline}) = \frac{ N(\textrm{trigger}~\&\&~\textrm{offline}) }{ N(\textrm{offline}) }
  \label{eq:trig_eff_wrt_offline}
\end{equation}
is lower than 100\% and needs to be evaluated. Figure~\ref{fig:trig_eff_MC} display the efficiency curves from MC, for the signal $H\to\gamma\gamma_d$ and for two background processes with $E_T^{miss}$, i.e. $Z(\to\nu\nu)\gamma$
and $W(\to\mu\nu)\gamma$. The inclusive $Z(\to\nu\nu/\ell\ell)\gamma$ case is shown as well. The efficiency curve for $W(\to\mu\nu)\gamma$ is shown for events with one reconstructed muon: as the muon is not considered for trigger-level $E_T^{miss}$ evaluation, also the offline $E_T^{miss}$ and $m_T$ are computed treating the muon as invisible.

However the efficiency curves for the signal exhibit large differences. The reason has been tracked down to the different kinematics of the signal, where the value of $m_T$ is constrained in an interval $[90;150]~\mathrm{GeV}$. To prove that this is indeed the reason, the efficiency curves have been extracted again only for events with $m_T<150~\mathrm{GeV}$: the result is visible in Figure~\ref{fig:trig_eff_MC_mT150}. Indeed, now the efficiency curves for $H\to\gamma\gamma_d$ and $Z(\to\nu\nu)\gamma$ do match.
The efficiency curve for \mT\  does not exhibit the usual smooth turn-on; instead, it shows an irregular structure with an initial plateau around 100-150 GeV and a second plateau at higher values. This behavior originates from correlations between \mT\  and both the \ETmiss\  and \pTy. The two plateaus correspond to two distinct event populations depending on whether the photon and \ETmiss\  are back-to-back (low \dphimety), or more aligned (high \dphimety). Applying a requirement on $\dphimety>1.25$, consistent with the selection applied in the SR, removes this effect, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:trig_eff_MC_dphi}

In the high \dphimety\ region, the trigger efficiency curves as a function of \mT\  are relatively similar for the signal and the considered background processes. This drop in efficiency is reflected on the efficiencies as a function of \pTy\  and \ETmiss. For this reason, these efficiencies are also shown in the region with \mT\  below 150 GeV in figure \ref{fig:trig_eff_MC_dphi_lowmt}. The efficiency for the $Z\gamma$ sample is overall slightly lower than for $W\gamma$ and the signal, while the signal and $W\gamma$ exhibit very similar behavior. Therefore, we safely assume that any SF extracted for $W\gamma$ can be applied to the signal as well.

To assess the accuracy of the trigger simulation, the efficiency curves from MC must be compared to those derived from data. To do so, a baseline muon trigger is used, and the used final state contains a photon and a muon, which is treated as invisible in the evaluation of $E_T^{miss}$ and $m_T$. The efficiencies to be compared are defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \epsilon(\textrm{trigger}~|~\textrm{offline}~\&\&~\mu\textrm{-trigger}) = \frac{ N(\textrm{trigger}~\&\&~\textrm{offline}~\&\&~\mu\textrm{-trigger}) }{ N(\textrm{offline}~\&\&~\mu\textrm{-trigger}) }
  \label{eq:trig_eff_wrt_offline_basetrigger}
\end{equation}
Data must be compared to the total background, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:trig_eff_data_2023} and ~\ref{fig:trig_eff_data_2024} for 2023 and 2024 dataset respectively. Offline selections include preselections with the additional requirement of the presence of one muon and $\dphimety>1.25$. The dominant background for events with one muon is $W\gamma$, with non negligible contribution from \jfakey\ which are part of the total background shown in magenta color. 
For both datasets there is good agreement, well within statistical uncertainty, between data and $W\gamma$, as well as with the total background. We conclude that no SF, nor associated systematic uncertainty, is needed.
