\subsection{Analysis trigger}
The analysis trigger is defined with the following criteria:
\begin{itemize}
    \item 1 $\gamma$ Tight with $p_T^{\gamma} > 50$ GeV
    \item $p_T^{\text{miss}} > 70$ GeV
    \item $m_T(\gamma, p_T^{\text{miss}}) > 80$ GeV
\end{itemize}
The effect of these selections is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:minv-anatrig}, showing a significant difference in the behavior of the $\gamma\mu$ channel compared to the $\gamma e$ channel in the sidebands. 
This misalignment arises from the trigger level selection on $p_T^{\text{miss}}$, which corresponds to an offline selection on $p_T^{miss, no \mu}$.
As a result, a larger number of events pass the selection in the $\gamma\mu$ channel, since the trigger does not detect the muon, treating it as invisible. \\
Since the background subtraction method relies on the assumption that the two channels exhibit similar behaviors, this discrepancy must be properly treted to recover the expected distributions.
Hence, specific selections are applied to the $\gamma\mu$ channel alone, in order to realign its distribution to the $\gamma e$ channel. 
Two strategies are explored: applying higher thresholds on $p_T^{\text{miss}}$, or trying to reproduce the effect of the trigger-level $p_T^{\text{miss}}$ by taking into account the muon momentum. \\

  \label{fig:minv-anatrig}

The first strategy tests three increasing $p_T^{\text{miss}}$ selections.
As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ptmisscut-anatrig}, the additional cut flattens the $\gamma\mu$ distribution in the upper sideband, leading to a better agreement with the shape of the $\gamma e$ events.
However, the transition between the approximately flat sidebands occurs beneath the invariant mass peak, where a steep slope is observed.
This behavior weakens the reliability of the method, as it impacts the region where background subtraction is performed.

  }
  \hfill
  \subfloat[$p_T^{\text{miss}} >100~\mathrm{GeV}$]{

  \hfill
  \subfloat[$p_T^{\text{miss}} >120~\mathrm{GeV}$]{
    \includegraphics[width=0.32\textwidth]{plot_efy/trigger/canvas_anatrigger_minv_cutphi_met120.pdf}
  }
  \caption{Invariant mass distribution in the $\gamma e$ (blue) and $\gamma \mu$ (red) channels when the analysis trigger is applied together with a $p_T^{\text{miss}}$ selection on the $\gamma\mu$ channel.}
  \label{fig:ptmisscut-anatrig}
\end{figure}

In the second strategy, the effect of the trigger-level $p_T^{\text{miss}}$ is approximated by combining $p_T^{\text{miss, no } \mu}$ and $p_T^{\mu}$, using the relation:
$$p_T^{\text{miss}} = p_T^{\text{miss, no } \mu}- p_T^{\mu}$$
Depending on the angle between these two vectors, events that pass the $p_T^{\text{miss, no } \mu}$ threshold may fall below the cut once the vector sum is computed.
This situation is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:plot-ptimiss}, where the excluded region below the trigger threshold is represented by the grey area. \\
To account for this effect, a selection is applied on the angle $\Phi(p_T^{\text{miss, no } \mu}, p_T^{\mu})$, in order to exclude events where the vectors are closely aligned (i.e., where $\cos\Phi$ is close to one).
The results for three angular selections are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:phicut-anatrig}, showing the same problem as the first strategy. \\

\subsection{Trigger efficiency on electrons and photons}

In order to evaluate the analysis trigger effect on photons and electrons, higher offline selections are applied on the other variables that define the trigger, $p_T^{\text{miss}}$ and $m_T$, in order to cover their impact on the event selection. \\
Several configurations of increasing thresholds are tested:
\begin{itemize}
     \item $p_T^{\text{miss}} > $ 70, 100, 150, 200, 300, 400 GeV
      \item $m_T > $ 80, 100, 150, 200 GeV
\end{itemize}
The best selections for each channel is determined by maximizing the trigger efficiency as a function of transverse momentum, $\epsilon(p_T)$. \\
All tested configurations are presented separately for each channel.
